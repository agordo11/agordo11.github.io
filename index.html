Technical Thursday: xDeception

March 24, 2022
In this week's Technical Thursday I write about pitcher deception described as "one of baseball final unquantified frontiers."

I’ve decided to start doing Technical Thursday’s in addition to technical Tuesday’s during the weeks where I have a lot of baseball on my mind (most weeks lol). Today I will introduce the project I dedicate most of my time, xDeception: Quantifying Pitcher Biomechanical Uniqueness.

What is Pitcher Deception? Deception is a very common term in the baseball world that essentially is coined when a pitcher delivery is uniquely suited to deceive a hitter. Hitters naturally predict how a pitch will move and where they expect it be based off what they’ve seen in the past. So, if a pitcher is able to be unique in the way he delivers the ball versus the average he is more deceptive than someone who delivers the ball to the catcher in a more average fashion.

What already exists? There has been a ton of work put in to quantifying a pitchers unique ability to make a pitch move. This is called the pitcher’s stuff. There are a wide variety of stuff metrics that consider different things, but they all only model for uniqueness for what happens after the ball is released. xDeception considers only what happens prior to ball release.

It started as an introductory project this past summer where I evenly weighted the top 3 parts I thought were most influential; extension, hidden ball ratio and deviation from average arm slot.

Extension is how far the pitcher’s hand is from the rubber when the ball is released. If a pitcher can achieve and high extension then the ball has to travel less distance making the ball appear to be thrown harder than it actually was. Hidden ball ratio is the ratio of frames that the pitcher is able to hide the ball from the hitter from when the hands separate from the glove until the pitcher’s front foot (plant foot) hits the ground. The theory behind arm slot deviation is a bit more complex. Arm slot is the angle at which base of the throwing arm is to the ground. The amount of time between front foot plant and ball release is the shortest of major time periods in the delivery so hitters have to use past experience the most during this period to predict where the pitch will come out. Arm slot deviation models for how much a pitcher is able to deviate from the average change of arm slot from foot plant to ball release.

Currently I’ve taken a more data driven approach that is clustering for outliers. This removes most of my personal bias because allowing for a machine learning model to learn the data unsupervised and find anomalies in the data that I give to the model.

In my current model extension has a very low effect on causing outlier, so I’ve removed it. The seemingly endless process I’ve been going through recently is giving the model certain features (a feature is when I take a list of location or velocities over a time period and extract it into a single value) that I believe to play the largest role and seeing how it effects the strength of the model. I’m very excited for the potential of this project as it has been described as “one of baseball’s final unquantified frontiers.”

Subtraction

March 23, 2022
How can you get what you want by subtracting?

There’s so much talk about what the best supplements are and what supplements can be added to our diets to improve our health, ability to recover or ability to put on muscle and lose fat. The free market has given us hundreds and hundreds of potential answers, but a better question is, what can be removed from our diets or routines to get what we want. This applies to all part of life.

As motivated humans we naturally are looking for things that we can add to our lives to make things better but often adding things just leads to the desire to add more things later. What is almost always overlooked is that you can achieve the same thing by removing something.

There are endless examples, like if your trying to allow your muscles to recover better. Instead of adding a protein supplement what if you spend less time on your phone so you can sleep more. If you’re trying to make more money, instead of working more, what if you spend less. It’s a very powerful question to ask yourself, how can I get what I want by subtracting?

I’ve taken this approach in practicing baseball. I go over it more in depth in my March 15 blog post, but the gist of it is when adding things to my daily routine, all the benefits that drill or stretch was giving me was hurting my decision fatigue more than the drill was helping me physically. So, maybe adding these supplements and our various other wants are just fuel for overthought.

I don’t think I’m alone with having thoughts along the lines of if I only had this then I could do this. These thoughts just lead us to think we can’t take action towards our passions because the first path we see to success is not within our means. I go to this a lot, but with good reason. The creativity involved in the adventure to excellence is one of the most fulfilling parts of it.

Looking back at my time playing JUCO baseball, the most beautiful aspect of it was how much we were able to achieve with how little resources that we had. During the height of the COVID lockdown, my teammates and I were desperately trying to find somewhere where we could practice. We couldn’t use our own field, we got kicked off of multiple high school fields and there were no private facilities that would let us use their buildings. So, we threw plyo balls (squishy balls filled with sand) into a mattress in our apartment and threw in the alley behind our apartment building. Funny enough, looking back, there was never any other period of time where I improved more than for the few months be creative making due with what we had.

There’s definitely something to be learned from all the wealthy people who aren’t happy. In one of my favorite movies, Fight Club, there’s a very famous quote, “The things you own end up owning you.” Likewise, the things you want end up owning you. If we allow the things we don’t have to dictate how we take action on our passions, they are owning us. So, instead of looking for something to get in order to get a desired outcome. How can you get what you want with what you already have or by removing or getting rid of something?

Technical Tuesday #2 - Imitation and it's application to player development

March 22, 2022
Day 14, two weeks straight and episode two Technical Tuesday!

In my March 16 blog post, The Power of Understanding Desire, I talked a little about how we are all born natural imitators. It’s one of the defense mechanisms we have developed throughout our evolution. The instant a baby is born they look to their mother and begin to understand what should be valued by what the mother is giving her attention to.

I know your definitely asking yourself, today is the second edition of Technical Tuesday, how is Ari relating all of this to baseball development? Well here’s how.

One of my favorite books on the mental side of sports is The Inner Game of Tennis by Tim Galloway. In the book, he talks about how we have two selves. Self 1 is the conscious mind, while self 2 is subconscious mind. Self 1 is in control when we try to make our bodies complete deliberate movements, while flow-state is when our self 2 takes over.

Pitchers mechanical movements are extremely important and it often leads to building too large of a relationship with self 1. When in self 1, there is no flow, so the mind is actively trying to activate and deactivate muscles, and 99.9% of the time will get it wrong.

This is where self 2 comes in and we take advantage of that natural defense mechanism. If the pitcher knows how they want a movement to look like very clearly, they allow the imitation to happen.

Look at Tiger Woods and his kid. His kid almost perfectly imitates the movements of his father’s golf swing and is playing golf at a level unparalleled with other kids his age. I think this also plays a large role in the team setting.

Something I’ve observed from the various places I’ve played at is when all the players first arrive on campus, the standard deviation of their peak velocity tends to be much larger than it is at the end of the year. I have no data to back it up but it’s just something I’ve noticed and I think it all has to do with imitation.

I haven’t yet decided if this is because the players will imitate the average or imitate the best players on the team. I think it depends on what level they are at and what the priorities are. But it’s definitely a very interesting observation that I wonder its’ effect at a larger scale.

I will write again about this again in the future. Very curious to how this can be actively manipulated by coaches to improve skill level across the board.

I wonder how we can all use this principle of present danger to absolutely fall in love with the process and become completely present in the act of chasing our goals. Maybe we can trick our mind into thinking we are more often in life or death scenarios, getting rid of the fear of failure and swapping it for an absolute necessity to win or fulfill our dreams.

Learning Skills

March 18, 2022
"Everything is a scam." - Riva Tez

I used to tell myself I suck at reading, I suck at writing, I suck at this or that. When at the very moment that may have been true but the connotation of telling ourselves that we suck at something is very negative and removes all motivation. Instead we should increasingly embrace the adventure to excellence at the skills we want to learn that may not come naturally.

I wanted to start a blog for years but never did because I kept telling myself I sucked at writing and look now. I’m rolling. Being a math major I see this a lot. People always asking me for math help saying they suck at math but that is just because they’ve been telling that to themselves all their lives. All parts of the brain can be developed, it just takes time and effort. There is tons of scientific research on this. The one study I always go back to is on London Taxi drivers before the age of GPS. For background, London has one of the most complicated and confusing layouts of any city in the world and these taxi drivers were expected to memorize all the ins and outs of it. The study measured the size of these new taxi driver’s hippocampus, the part that is responsible for spatial recognition, before they started driving and after a period of six months. The size of all these taxi driver’s spatial recognition sector of their brain all increased in size by a significant percentage.

Moral of the story is the things we tell ourselves are often false. We can all learn any skill we want. The barriers to entry are all just scams. Do what you want to.

Technical Tuesday: 80/20 your daily throwing routine, a potential cure for the y**s

March 15, 2022
Technical Tuesday is where I write about a player development topic that is on my mind or a part of a project that I'm working on every tuesday.

The 80/20 principle suggests that you get at least 80% of the resulted outcome from at maximum 20% of inputs or effort. This has typically has stayed in the world of business and economics but it is very interesting to consider in terms of player development. The most obvious part that stands out to me is nutrition. I’ve known so many players that dominate their supplement routine but let the actual food they eat to be far below average. Eating 3 to 4, healthy and balanced meals per day is the 20% that gets at least 80% of results, while protein powder, creatine, mass-gainer, tart cherry juice and the list goes on forever is what gets those last few percentage points. I know I’m making a big claim here, but I think this principle is a key to getting rid of the dreaded y’s.

I won’t say it.

No, I won’t say it.

Fine, the yips.

The largest commonality I’ve seen in myself and others that have been yipped is their pre and post throw routines. Typically, the guys who are in this situation are the ones who take the longest to complete their routines. I made this realization by accident. As I began to lose enjoyment this past fall as I struggled with command to the point where I lost all enjoyment, I decided to say fuck it and cut almost everything out of my routine except the few things that would get me at least 80% of the way. To be clear, I’m not suggesting to lower your ethic. I’m suggesting to direct the high work ethic to the things that are actually important.

For me, the only things I kept was a dynamic warm up, bands and an extremely limited number of plyo throws. This included control over what my daily throwing regiment was. I began doing whatever throwing my throwing partner wanted to do. This naturally removed everything from my routine that allowed for overthinking. In other words, this removed all decision fatigue from my baseball life, allowing me to allocate my decision-making skills the most important parts of my routine without even having to think about it.

To the players out there struggling with playing time, command or anything of the sorts, I urge you to try the few things that get you most of the way, instead of getting lost in the 80% of things that only get you 20% of the way to your goals.

My Latest Habit

March 14, 2022
What I've learned from so far from 50 hours in meditation in 2022

At the beginning of 2022, I committed to a minimum of 30 minutes of meditation every day. All my life I’ve been a perpetual overthinker and it has always been more negative for me than positive. This overthinking of pretty much everything combined with my analytical brain was the largest factor in maintaining all of my anxieties and lead to problems in many parts of my life. At the end of 2021 I started to lose fulfilment and enjoyment for the playing baseball, the thing I love to do the most, and my social anxiety had been the at the worst it had ever been. I knew I needed to address my constant absence from the present moment.

I wasn’t a complete beginner to meditation but in the past the motivation behind it was to improve my ability at a skill instead of reconnecting with each and every moment. This difference has proved to be very significant. The hardest part about getting into meditation for beginners, besides any stigma they have towards it, is that there is very little immediate feedback. Back when I was doing it to as an attempt to improve my ability on the mound I had to force myself to do it and I could only consistently manage 10-minute sessions. Now, it’s become one of my favorite activities I do every day and my 30-minute session every morning flies by. The skill of being able to do absolutely nothing and enjoy it has completely changed all aspects of my life. I now love to go to practice every day again and my social anxiety has completely dissolved.

“How can you do anything if you can’t even do nothing.” If you’re not good at doing nothing, how can you expect to be at your best doing something? Being able to reconnect with the present and your inner being at any moment boosts creativity, focus and productivity to a level you would never expect possible while dissolving fears, anxieties and worries. This is why meditation has become such a important part of my daily routine.

If you want to get into it, I recommend not using any guided meditation. For me, guided mediation is just another curtain over the present moment. So, I recommend simply sitting completely still in a comfortable chair and setting a timer for 30-minutes and observe all the thoughts that come into your mind. A very important aspect to realize is that there is no such thing as failing in meditation. By sitting still and listening to your thoughts, it is automatically successful. I think you find that the 30-minutes goes by much faster than you’d think.

The movies behind wise eyes

March 12, 2022
A bit of a longer and deeper post today.

My biggest fear is at the end of my life looking back with regret that I didn’t build what I dreamt of building or experience the things I dreamt of experiencing. Recently I had the opportunity to listen to someone speak about their time playing baseball at the same place I’m currently at and the look in his eyes left a permanent imprint in my brain. The amount of light he had in his eyes immediately reminded me of the look in my grandpa’s eyes after my grandmother died. I could never put my finger on why that look in his eyes was so powerful until I had the opportunity to listen to this man speak. Anatomically, the iris, the colored part of the eye, is a perforated muscular curtain. These perforations are like pixels that make up the movie screen as a portal into someone’s perspective and experience. If present enough to notice, the movie shown behind wise eyes can be an insanely valuable teacher. The powerful emotions combined with wisdom are what makes these movies viewable in ultra-high definition.

It was funny because he never mentioned a word about any actual games. Nothing about how well he played, nothing about what he had accomplished and nothing about how the team played or what they had accomplished. He talked about all the shenanigans he did with the people he cared about the most and the work him and his team struggled through to start building UNC Charlotte into what it has become today. At the time they didn’t even have a baseball field. They would go practice at a high school field and come back after and work tirelessly trying to make their own field.

Now thinking back to my time at JUCO when we were forced to make the most out of the little that we had, especially during COVID. When we got back in the late summer of 2020, our field was in shambles. It looked like it hadn’t been played on in decades. The six-foot-tall weeds spreading across the warning track was only the start, but as a team, we spent a week working our tails off pulling every single weed from the outfield and killing everything that was making the infield invisible.

It’s the things that are scratched and clawed for that really stick out in memories. I remember these periods of comfort in my life, like going on a relaxing vacation, long stretches of playing video games or going out and getting drunk to escape something and just exiting them feeling worse than I did before. On the flip side, the times of weeding the field at juco, river canoeing through the cold and rain on camping trips, staying up far too late working on my passions, strapping a portable mound to the top of my car and throwing plyos in my $200/month apartment are what have created true fulfilment in my life.

So, moral of the story, the more people that are building something, the better the world will be. My hope is for the future is for more people do the things and go the places that make them feel most alive.

A beautiful aspect of pitching

March 9, 2022
Pitching is my first love. Here's part of what makes it unique that I've recently come to realize.

Pitchers all have relatively the same number of throws in their arms. This is my math brain speaking theoretically.

Unlike pretty much all other sports, pitchers are extremely limited in the amount they can physically practice their skill each day. Really, they can make over 100 throws twice a week at most to keep injury risk manageable and these other days they can only throw between 30 and 60 throws.

That all adds up to 500–750 throws per week depending on role at the highest throwing volume part of the year. A basketball player can shoot over 10,000 shots per week and a wide receiver can catch that many passes a week.

Theoretically the best pitchers are the one who are most efficient with their throws and practice the smartest. This is where science and pitching first collide and the crazy part is it is just on the surface of where they do.

It’s beautiful. Who can be most efficient?
